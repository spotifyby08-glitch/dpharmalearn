Chapter 1 Pharmaceutics I

History & Development of Pharmacy

From ancient healers to modern pharmaceutical science — a comprehensive overview of how pharmacy evolved through centuries.

Download PDF  Watch Video

~20 min read

1. Introduction to Pharmacy

Pharmacy is the science and practice of discovering, producing, preparing, dispensing, reviewing, and monitoring medications. It is a health profession that links health sciences with chemical sciences and aims to ensure the safe and effective use of pharmaceutical drugs.

The word "pharmacy" is derived from the Greek word pharmakeia (φαρμακεία), meaning "use of drugs, medicines, and potions." The evolution of pharmacy is deeply intertwined with the history of medicine and human civilization.

Key Definition

Pharmacy is the art, practice, and profession of choosing, preparing, storing, compounding, and dispensing medicine to optimize patient outcomes and to ensure that patients use medicine appropriately to achieve the best possible health outcomes.

2. Ancient History of Pharmacy

2.1 Prehistoric Period

The use of medicinal plants dates back to prehistoric times. Archaeological evidence suggests that Neanderthals may have used plants like yarrow, groundsel, and ephedra for medicinal purposes approximately 60,000 years ago.

Early humans observed that animals would seek out certain plants when ill — a phenomenon called zoopharmacognosy. This observation led humans to experiment with plants for healing purposes.

2.2 Ancient Egypt (3000–400 BCE)

The Egyptians were among the earliest civilizations to document medicinal practices. The Ebers Papyrus (circa 1550 BCE) is one of the oldest known medical texts, containing over 700 prescriptions and remedies using substances like aloe, opium, castor oil, mint, and pomegranate.

Egyptian priests who specialized in medicine were also early precursors of pharmacists. They controlled the preparation and administration of medicinal substances within temple complexes.

Historical Fact

The Ebers Papyrus (1550 BCE) is considered one of the world's oldest medical documents and contains 877 prescriptions for various ailments including heart disease, stomach problems, and eye infections.

2.3 Mesopotamia & Babylonia

The Mesopotamians developed extensive records of medicinal herbs. The Code of Hammurabi (circa 1754 BCE) included regulations about the practice of medicine. Clay tablets from this era list hundreds of medicinal plants, minerals, and animal products used in treatment.

3. Ayurvedic Pharmacy in India

India has one of the world's oldest medical traditions. Ayurveda (meaning "Science of Life") dates back approximately 5,000 years. Major Ayurvedic texts include:

  • Charaka Samhita – Focuses on internal medicine, describing 341 plant-based medicines, 177 animal-based medicines, and 64 mineral preparations.
  • Sushruta Samhita – Emphasizes surgery and describes hundreds of medicinal plants with therapeutic uses.
  • Ashtanga Hridayam – A condensed version of Charaka and Sushruta, widely used in Kerala tradition.

Indian pharmacists of ancient times were called Dravyaguna Vaidyas, skilled in identifying, preparing, and dispensing medicinal herbs and formulations.

4. Greek & Roman Contributions

Ancient Greece made tremendous contributions to pharmacy through systematic observation and documentation.

  • Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) – "Father of Medicine," emphasized natural causes of disease and introduced rational medicine.
  • Dioscorides (40–90 CE) – Wrote "De Materia Medica," which catalogued over 600 plants, their medicinal properties, and preparation methods. This text remained a reference for over 1,500 years.
  • Galen (129–216 CE) – Developed "Galenical preparations" — methods of extracting active principles from plants using solvents. He is considered the Father of Pharmacy.

Father of Pharmacy

Claudius Galen (129–216 CE) is considered the Father of Pharmacy. His systematic approach to drug preparation, including extraction and purification techniques, laid the foundation for modern pharmaceutical sciences.

5. Medieval Pharmacy (Arab Golden Age)

Arab scholars preserved and expanded Greek knowledge during the Islamic Golden Age (8th–13th centuries). Key contributions include:

  • Avicenna (Ibn Sina, 980–1037 CE) – His "Canon of Medicine" described 760 drugs and became a standard medical text in Europe for 600 years.
  • Arab pharmacists (called Saydalanis) established the first independent pharmacies, separating pharmacy from medicine.
  • They introduced new dosage forms: syrups, electuaries, conserves, and medicated wines.

6. Development of Modern Pharmacy

6.1 Apothecary Era (14th–18th Century)

The term "apothecary" (derived from the Greek apotheke, meaning storehouse) referred to early pharmacists. In England, the Worshipful Society of Apothecaries was established in 1617, separating pharmacy from medicine as a distinct profession.

6.2 Industrial Revolution (19th Century)

The development of chemistry and the industrial revolution transformed pharmacy. Key milestones include:

  • 1805 – Morphine isolated by Friedrich Sertürner (first alkaloid)
  • 1820 – United States Pharmacopeia (USP) first published
  • 1847 – American Pharmaceutical Association (APhA) founded
  • 1899 – Aspirin synthesized and marketed by Bayer

6.3 20th Century – Pharmaceutical Renaissance

The 20th century saw explosive growth in pharmaceutical science:

  • 1928 – Alexander Fleming discovers Penicillin
  • 1940s – Mass production of antibiotics begins
  • 1960s – Clinical pharmacy emerges as a discipline
  • 1970s – Drug information and patient counseling emphasis grows
  • 1990s – Pharmacogenomics and personalized medicine emerges

7. Pharmacy in India – Regulatory History

Modern pharmacy regulation in India developed through several milestones:

  • 1940 – Pharmacy Act enacted, establishing the foundation for pharmacy education and regulation.
  • 1948 – Pharmacy Council of India (PCI) established to regulate pharmacy education.
  • 1954 – Drugs and Cosmetics Rules framed to control drug manufacturing and sale.
  • 1970 – Patent Act limits product patents on drugs, making medicines affordable.
  • 2004 – Schedule H1 introduced for prescription-only drugs.

Pharmacy Council of India (PCI)

The PCI regulates pharmacy education and practice in India. It approves D-Pharm and B.Pharm courses and ensures uniformity of education standards across the country.

8. Chapter Summary

Pharmacy has evolved from the use of crude plant remedies in prehistoric times to a complex, science-based discipline. Key milestones include ancient Egyptian papyri, Ayurvedic traditions, Greek systematic medicine, Arab pharmaceutical advancements, and the industrial revolution that led to modern drug manufacturing.

The Pharmacy Act 1940 and establishment of PCI are cornerstones of pharmacy regulation in India. Understanding this history helps pharmacists appreciate their important role in healthcare.
