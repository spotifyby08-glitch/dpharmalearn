Introduction, Errors & Impurities in Pharmaceutical Substances

Chapter 1 — Pharmaceutical Chemistry I | D-Pharma 1st Year

1. Introduction to Pharmaceutical Chemistry

Pharmaceutical Chemistry is the branch of chemistry concerned with the design, synthesis, analysis, and quality control of drugs and pharmaceutical substances. It combines principles of organic chemistry, inorganic chemistry, biochemistry, and pharmacology to ensure that drugs are safe, effective, and of acceptable quality before they reach the patient.

Scope: Pharmaceutical chemistry covers: (a) study of physical and chemical properties of drug substances; (b) identification, characterisation, and structure determination of drugs; (c) synthesis of new drug molecules and chemical modification of existing drugs to improve activity; (d) analysis and quality control using pharmacopoeial (IP, BP, USP) and non-pharmacopoeial methods; (e) study of drug stability, degradation products, and impurity profiling; (f) development of analytical methods (HPLC, UV, titration) for drug testing.

Importance in pharmacy practice: Every drug released for patient use must meet defined standards of identity (is it the correct drug?), purity (is it free from harmful impurities?), and potency (does it contain the right amount of active substance?). Pharmaceutical chemistry provides the scientific basis for establishing and testing these standards.

Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR) is a key concept — it describes how changes in a drug's chemical structure affect its biological activity, toxicity, and pharmacokinetics. Understanding SAR allows medicinal chemists to design more potent, selective, and safer drugs. For example, modifying the acetyl group in aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) to different acyl groups changes its anti-inflammatory potency. SAR is the foundation of modern rational drug design.

Pharmacopoeias — Quality Standards

A pharmacopoeia is an official book of standards for medicines. The Indian Pharmacopoeia (IP) is published by the Indian Pharmacopoeia Commission (IPC) and sets legally enforceable standards for drugs manufactured and sold in India. Other major pharmacopoeias include the British Pharmacopoeia (BP), United States Pharmacopeia (USP), and European Pharmacopoeia (EP). They specify tests for identification, purity, and assay of each official drug.

2. Errors in Pharmaceutical Analysis

In any quantitative analysis, the measured result rarely equals the true value exactly. The difference between the measured value and the true value is called an error. Understanding and minimising errors is essential for reliable analytical results.

Error = Measured Value − True Value

Errors are classified into three main types:

2.1 Determinate Errors (Systematic Errors)

These are errors that have a definite, assignable cause and always affect results in the same direction (always too high or always too low). They are reproducible and can be detected and eliminated once identified.

  • Operational errors: Poor technique — incorrect titration endpoint, incomplete washing of precipitate, loss of material during transfer
  • Instrumental errors: Uncalibrated balances, faulty burettes, inaccurate pipettes, temperature errors in volumetric glassware
  • Reagent errors: Impure reagents, degraded standards, incorrect concentration of solutions
  • Method errors: Inherent flaws in the analytical method — e.g., a reaction that is never 100% complete, or a side reaction that consumes part of the analyte

2.2 Indeterminate Errors (Random Errors)

These are errors due to uncontrollable, unpredictable fluctuations in measurements — slight variations in temperature, humidity, observer's reading of a scale (parallax error), electrical fluctuations in instruments. They affect results randomly — sometimes high, sometimes low — and cannot be completely eliminated. They can be minimised by taking multiple readings and calculating the mean. Statistical methods (standard deviation, confidence intervals) are used to quantify random errors.

2.3 Gross Errors (Blunders)

These are large errors caused by analyst mistakes — misreading a burette, recording a wrong number, using the wrong reagent, or arithmetic mistakes in calculations. They are not systematic and not random — they are simply human errors. Results affected by gross errors are discarded and the experiment repeated.

2.4 Accuracy and Precision

  • Accuracy: How close the measured value is to the true value. A measure of correctness. Expressed as percentage error or percentage recovery.
  • Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other (reproducibility). Does NOT indicate whether results are near the true value.

Accuracy vs Precision — Exam Classic

High accuracy + High precision: All shots hit the bullseye (ideal)

Low accuracy + High precision: All shots clustered together but away from bullseye (systematic error present)

Low accuracy + Low precision: Shots scattered all over (random error dominant)

2.5 Methods to Minimise Errors

  • Calibrate all instruments (balances, burettes, pipettes) regularly
  • Use freshly prepared, standardised reagents and solutions
  • Perform blank corrections — run a blank (no analyte) to subtract background
  • Run parallel determinations (duplicate/triplicate) and average results
  • Use internal standards in chromatographic methods
  • Ensure proper training of analysts and strict adherence to SOP (Standard Operating Procedures)

3. Impurities in Pharmaceutical Substances

An impurity is any component of a drug substance or drug product that is not the intended chemical entity. Even trace amounts of impurities can be clinically significant — affecting safety, efficacy, and stability of the drug.

3.1 Sources of Impurities

  • Starting materials: Impurities already present in raw materials used for synthesis
  • Synthesis by-products: Intermediates, side-reaction products, unreacted starting materials left over from manufacturing
  • Degradation: Breakdown of the drug molecule due to heat, light, moisture, oxidation, or hydrolysis during storage
  • Equipment: Leaching of metals or contaminants from reactors, pipes, and containers during manufacturing
  • Residual solvents: Organic solvents used in synthesis or purification that are not completely removed
  • Packaging: Migration of plasticisers, inks, or adhesives from primary packaging into the product

3.2 Classification of Impurities (ICH Q3A/Q3B)

The International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) classifies impurities into three categories:

A. Organic Impurities

  • Arise from the synthesis process or degradation of the drug substance
  • Include: starting materials, intermediates, by-products, degradation products
  • Examples: Salicylic acid in aspirin (hydrolysis product); 4-aminophenol in paracetamol (degradation product — toxic)
  • Detected and quantified by HPLC, TLC, GC

B. Inorganic Impurities

  • Derived from manufacturing processes, equipment, or raw materials
  • Include: Heavy metals (Pb, As, Hg, Cd), chlorides, sulphates, iron, inorganic salts, catalysts (palladium, nickel), filter aids
  • Detected by pharmacopoeial limit tests (covered in Chapter 2)
  • Heavy metals are of particular concern due to toxicity even at very low concentrations (ppm level)

C. Residual Solvents

Organic solvents used during synthesis that remain in the final product. ICH Q3C classifies them into three classes:

3.3 Effects of Impurities and Their Control

Even trace amounts of impurities can have serious consequences on drug quality and patient safety:

  • Toxicity: Heavy metals (As, Pb, Hg, Cd) are highly toxic even at ppm levels — they accumulate in body tissues causing kidney damage, neurotoxicity, and carcinogenicity. Arsenic above 2 ppm in any drug is unacceptable.
  • Reduced efficacy: If an inert impurity makes up a significant percentage of the drug's weight, the actual active content per dose is less than labelled — resulting in underdosing.
  • Allergic reactions: Degradation products of penicillin (penicilloic acid, penilloic acid) are the major cause of penicillin hypersensitivity and anaphylaxis — the impurities, not the drug itself, are the allergens.
  • Altered stability: Metal ion impurities (Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺) catalyse oxidative degradation of drugs, causing premature breakdown, colour change, and loss of potency during storage.
  • Pharmacological side effects: 4-aminophenol in paracetamol causes methaemoglobinaemia; free salicylic acid in aspirin causes additional gastric irritation.

Control of impurities: Pharmacopoeias (IP, BP, USP, EP) set legally enforceable limits for each specified impurity in every drug monograph. Manufacturers must test every batch and demonstrate compliance before product release. ICH guidelines Q3A (impurities in new drug substances) and Q3B (impurities in drug products) govern global standards for impurity reporting, identification, and qualification thresholds.

4. Introduction to Limit Tests

A Limit Test is a semi-quantitative test that determines whether the amount of a specific impurity in a drug substance exceeds a defined maximum permissible level. It gives a pass/fail result — not the exact quantity.

Principle: The test solution (drug sample) is compared against a standard solution containing a known, permitted amount of the impurity. Both are treated with the same reagent under identical conditions. If the test solution produces equal or less colour/turbidity than the standard → test PASSES.

Limit tests described in IP: Chlorides, Sulphates, Iron, Heavy Metals, and Arsenic (Gutzeit method). These are covered in detail in Chapter 2.

General Precautions for Limit Tests

  • Use matched Nessler cylinders (identical flat-bottomed glass tubes, 50 mL) for fair visual comparison
  • Use freshly boiled and cooled ion-free distilled water
  • All glassware must be free from the ion being tested
  • Standard solutions must be freshly prepared
  • Compare both tubes against a white background in uniform light
  • Turbidity tests: view from top downward | Colour tests: view from the side
